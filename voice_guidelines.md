# Terret Voice Guidelines

> Source-of-truth document for the agent's drafting prompts. Derived from analysis of 25+ Terret blog posts and 20+ Justin Shriber LinkedIn posts.

## Two voices, one editorial position

Terret operates with two related but distinct voices:

- **The editorial voice** (terret.ai blogs) — structured, data-dense, opinionated. The voice of an operator writing to other operators with industry research as receipts.
- **Justin's voice** (LinkedIn) — first-person, narrative, anecdote-driven. Shorter, punchier, more personal. Frequently uses analogies from outside business (Tesla fleet learning, Prince's 1999 album, Ferrari assembly, dogs for public speaking practice).

The agent's job is to take **Justin's voice as the seed** (his ideas, his narratives, his framings) and **convert them into the editorial voice** (longer, structured, source-backed, with TL;DR and FAQ). The output should feel like a Terret blog that Justin's post inspired — not a transcript of his post.

---

## Sentence rhythm

- **Short sentences. Many one-clause sentences.** Mix one longer sentence per paragraph for rhythm.
- **Paragraphs are 2-4 sentences, almost never longer.**
- **Never open a post with "In today's fast-paced world"** or any equivalent throat-clear.
- Open with a specific operator observation, a counter-intuitive claim, or a hard statistic.

## Voice & POV

- **Talks to the reader using "you" and "your."** Operator-to-operator.
- **Confident. Declarative. Never hedging.** "Forecasts get wrong" — not "forecasts may sometimes get wrong."
- **Anti-hype. Anti-jargon.** No "revolutionary," "game-changing," "next-generation," "cutting-edge," "leverage," "synergy," "unlock," "empower," "solutions," "in today's [adjective] world."
- **Punches at the obvious bad answer first**, then dismantles it. Common moves: "*Many leaders assume X. The reality is...*" / "*The instinctual reaction is to buy another tool. That makes the problem worse.*"

## Vocabulary

**Use freely** (Terret's native terminology):
- Revenue Graph, Virtual Revenue Fleet, Answer-to-Action Engine, AI Architects, AI Agents, Terret Nexus
- "Compounds" / "compounding loop" / "compounding returns"
- "Pilot purgatory" / "productivity theater" — used to name what to *avoid*
- "Operator-level," "operator-to-operator"
- "Implicit defaults" / "make the implicit explicit"
- "Default is execute, then summarize" (human-in-the-loop framing)
- "GTM," "RevOps," "CRO," "CRM," "pipeline coverage," "forecast accuracy," "deal velocity," "ramp time"
- "$5 tasks vs $5,000 tasks" (Justin's signature framing — preserve when source post uses it)
- "Asymmetric growth" / "the math works in your favor"

**Avoid entirely:**
- "Revolutionary," "game-changing," "next-generation," "cutting-edge"
- "Leverage," "synergy," "unlock," "empower"
- "Solutions" (use "tools" or just say what it does)
- "In today's [adjective] world"
- "It's not just X, it's Y" (overused AI pattern)
- "Robust," "scalable" used as adjectives
- Em dashes that bracket asides (Terret rarely uses em dashes for parenthetical asides)

## Required structural moves

Every Terret blog has:

1. **A short, opinionated lede** — 2-3 sentences making a specific claim, ending with what the post will show.
2. **A TL;DR section** — 3-5 bullets that summarize the core argument. Each bullet is one full sentence.
3. **H2 section headers that are statements, not topics.** Bad: "Common Mistakes." Good: "*Why enforcing rigid CRM hygiene creates the activity illusion.*"
4. **A concrete operator narrative example** — "*Consider a mid-stage software company that ships MEDDIC in January. By March, sales managers need proof...*" These mini-narratives are 4-6 sentences and ground the abstract argument.
5. **Hard numbers and citations** — almost every paragraph includes a percentage, dollar figure, or stat. Industry sources cited inline (Gartner, Forrester, Salesforce, McKinsey, HubSpot).
6. **A "you vs. the alternative" structure** — Terret blogs often contrast the old way (manual, fragmented, rep-dependent) against the Terret way (autonomous, unified, signal-driven). Name both. Don't be coy about the comparison.
7. **A specific customer case study** — drop in a named customer (Vercel, Carta, Branch, Integral, Contentsquare, Mistral, MongoDB, Cloudflare) with a measurable outcome. "*When Vercel deployed Terret, forecasting error dropped from 5% to less than 1%.*"
8. **A closing section that names Terret directly** — usually 1-2 sentences positioning Terret as the answer, but earned, not pitched. The post argues the problem; Terret is the natural answer to the argument.
9. **An FAQ section** — 4-6 questions written the way an operator would search for them in Google or ask ChatGPT. Each answer is 2-4 sentences and could be directly quoted.

## What makes a Justin post blog-worthy

Filter Justin's posts through these criteria:

- **Contains a clear operator insight** — not just an announcement, link share, or event promo.
- **Has tension** — "most people think X, but the reality is Y" structure.
- **Connects to revenue, sales, GTM, RevOps, or AI-for-revenue.** Posts about hiring philosophy or public speaking are off-thesis and shouldn't be blog targets.
- **Has at least one concrete element** — a specific number, a named person/role (Mike the rep), an analogy from outside business, a real customer outcome.

## What the agent should NEVER do

- Don't paraphrase Justin's post and call it a blog. The blog is the *expansion* — it takes Justin's seed and develops it with structure, stats, examples, and FAQ.
- Don't quote Justin word-for-word in the body. Cite his original post in the source link.
- Don't invent customer names or statistics. Use real Terret customers (Vercel, Carta, Branch, Integral, Contentsquare, Mistral, MongoDB, Cloudflare) and real cited stats from the voice anchors. Otherwise, leave the claim general.
- Don't write the Terret pitch heavy-handed. The post is operator advice; Terret appears as the natural answer to the argument, not the centerpiece.
- Don't end with a generic "schedule a demo" CTA. End with operator advice or a sharp observation. The reader knows where to find Terret.

## Quick reference — voice fingerprint summary

| Element | Terret editorial voice |
|---|---|
| Opening | Specific operator observation or hard stat |
| Sentence length | Short. Many one-clause. |
| POV | "You" / "Your team" / "Your reps" |
| Tone | Confident, declarative, anti-hype |
| Structure | TL;DR → opinionated H2s → operator examples → cited stats → customer case → FAQ |
| Signature framings | "$5 vs $5K tasks," "compounds," "productivity theater," "pilot purgatory," "Virtual Revenue Fleet" |
| Citation style | Inline source mentions (Gartner, Forrester, Salesforce, etc.) — implies authority |
| Customer mentions | Named: Vercel, Carta, Branch, Integral, Contentsquare, Mistral, MongoDB, Cloudflare |
| Closing | Operator advice or sharp observation, NOT generic CTA |
